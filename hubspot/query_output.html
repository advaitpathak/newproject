CLO Activity

European CLOs Considered Riskier

According to numerous reports and as outlined by Global Capital, European CLOs are seeing a rise in CCC-rated exposure and loan defaults. Recovery rates are anticipated to be inferior to those seen in the past, despite the defaults increasing from an already low level. As per Fitch Ratings research, the trailing 12-month default rate to July 2023 in Europe was 1.7% but is expected to grow to 4.5% by the end of the year.

Increases in CCC buckets above the standard 7.5% threshold incorporated into CLOs would lead to haircuts to ensure the over-collateralization tests are passed. Interest is diverted from junior tranches to repay the principal to the most senior notes. According to Trepp data, European CLO CCC exposure is currently around 3.7% for both Moody’s and S&P-rated deals.

CMBS Loan Loss Report – Part 2: What Were the Five Largest Loan Losses in July 2023?

In the first part of the July 2023 monthly loss report, we covered the CMBS loan loss trends from the past 12 months. Here, Trepp provides a deeper dive into the five largest loans that resolved for a loss in July 2023.

See below for the five largest loans that resolved for a loss in July 2023 according to Trepp data.

Louis Joliet Mall (Joliet, Illinois)

The $85.0 million Louis Joliet Mall loan was the largest loan to resolve for a loss in July. The property includes almost 360,000 square feet of space in a Joliet, IL mall. The mall was built in 1978 and renovated in 2009.

The loan made up about 26.6% of the collateral behind UBSBB 2012-C2 prior to the resolution of the loan. That deal is part of CMBX 6.

The loan was transferred to special servicing in May 2020 for imminent monetary default due to the Covid-19 pandemic. We first wrote about this loan in an October 2020 edition of TreppWire, where we noted that negotiations for a deed-in-lieu (DIL) were in process. The property became REO in November 2021. According to servicing commentary, the REO asset was sold in June 2023.

The collateral was valued at $131.8 million in 2012 but that was lowered to $55.1 million in 2020. The value was upped to $65.2 million in 2021 but was later lowered to $48.2 million in 2022.

The Louis Joliet Mall was sold for $31.4 million. The gross liquidation proceeds came in below the most recent value. The proceeds totaled $41.3 million. After $11.5 million of liquidation expenses, that left the asset with a loss of $55.2 million.

CitiFinancial (Fort Mill, South Carolina)

The $21.3 million CitiFinancial loan was the largest office loan to resolve for a loss in July. This loan is backed by a 165,000 square-foot office built in 2005 in Fort Mill, South Carolina.

We first wrote about this loan in a January 2014 edition of TreppWire when Citibank made a layoff announcement for the default mortgage servicing group they were shutting down in Fort Mill. Citi had been the sole tenant in the building collateralizing the CitiFinancial loan. In a June 2015 edition of TreppWire, we noted that Walmart was temporarily occupying part of Citi’s space. The property later became the address for OneMain Financial.

The loan was first transferred to special servicing due to imminent maturity default in April 2017. It was returned to the master servicer in June 2018, with a modification pushing the maturity date out to December 2018. A second modification for the loan extended the maturity to April 2020. The loan was most recently transferred to the special servicer in April 2020 due to maturity default. A foreclosure complaint was filed in July 2020. In October 2022, the asset became REO. In July 2023, the REO asset was sold.

The DSCR (NCF) for the loan first fell below 1.0x in 2019 when it decreased from 1.30x to 0.93x. The occupancy was still at 100%. In the most recently reported financial period of 1H 2022, the DSCR was reported to be 0.02x, with the occupancy at 33%.

The collateral was valued at $35 million at securitization in 2007. The value has been lowered five times since then. In May 2023, the appraised value was cut from $14.1 million to $11.5 million.

Gross liquidation proceeds came in below the most recent value at $9.1 million. Liquidation expenses amounted to $5.5 million, leaving the asset with a loss of $17.7 million. Prior to resolution, the loan made up 9.33% of the remaining collateral behind GSMS 2007-GG10.

Coleman Marketplace (Danville, Virginia)

The $17.4 million Coleman Marketplace loan was the third largest loan to resolve for a loss in July. The collateral is a Danville, VA community shopping center containing a little more than 200,000 square feet of retail space primarily anchored by Marshall’s and Ross Dress for Less. The property was built in 2008.

In late 2018, we highlighted in TreppWire that the property behind this loan was expected to lose two of its top tenants. Dick’s Sporting Goods and Petco, which were the two stores scheduled to shutter, combined for about 30% of the collateral space.

In a March 2020 edition of TreppWire, we wrote that the loan failed to pay off on its balloon date and had been sent to special servicing. The asset became REO in July 2021. The REO was sold and closed in June 2023.

As a result of the departures of the two tenants going into 2019, occupancy fell from 86% to 53% from 2018 to 2019. The most recently reported occupancy was 54% in 2021. The DSCR (NCF) fell from 1.28x to 1.01x from 2018 to 2019. The DSCR fell to 0.53x in 2020 and slightly increased to 0.56x in 2021.

The asset backing the loan was appraised at about $22.1 million at securitization in 2016. The value was lowered to $15.9 million in 2020 and most recently was slightly raised to $16.4 million in 2022.

Liquidation proceeds came in slightly above the most recent value. The proceeds totaled $18.2 million. After $5.7 million of liquidation expenses, the asset was left with a loss of $4.9 million. The loan made up 29.93% of the remaining collateral behind BANC 2017-CRE2 before resolving.

470 Broadway (New York, New York)

The fourth-largest loan to resolve for a loss in July was the $17.4 million 470 Broadway retail loan. A 6,600-square-foot single-tenant street retail property in the SoHo neighborhood of New York City backs the loan. The property was built in 1910 and renovated in 2008. The property was 100% leased to retailer ALDO at origination with a lease expiring in December 2023.

The loan was transferred to the special servicer in May 2020 for imminent default due to the Covid-19 pandemic. ALDO had just filed for Chapter 15 and subsequently rejected the lease at the property. The property became REO in October 2022.

The value was initially appraised at securitization in 2012 at $29.2 million. In 2020, the value was reduced to $11.2 million. The appraised value fell twice more, to $5.65 million in 2021 and then to $4.1 million in 2022. The most recent appraised value in May 2023 was at $7.5 million, or roughly a quarter of the asset's original value.

Liquidation proceeds came in slightly above the most recent value. Proceeds totaled $8.4 million. After $7.2 million of liquidation expenses, that left the asset with $16.2 million in realized losses. Prior to resolution, the loan made up 9.56% of MSBAM 2012-C6.

Pocatello Square (Pocatello, Idaho)

The fifth-largest loan to resolve for a loss in July was the $17.1 million Pocatello Square retail loan. The loan is collateralized by a 138,925-square-foot multi-anchored community shopping center in Pocatello, ID. The property was built in 2006.

The retail property currently has four tenants. The two largest tenants at the property are Dick’s Sporting Goods, which occupies just under 48,000 square feet, and Ross Dress for Less, which occupies just over 30,000 square feet at the property. Both anchor tenants hold leases through January 2027.

The loan originally had an expected maturity date of April 2017. Since then, according to servicing commentary, the loan was transferred to the special servicer, with the borrower submitting a hardship letter indicating that it would not be able to pay off the loan and that it would transfer the title to the asset.

We first wrote about this loan in a July 2017 edition of TreppWire to note the closure of the Staples store. Staples was the third-largest tenant with almost 15% of the space. A foreclosure sale occurred in April 2018, and the property became REO.

In January 2022, we highlighted this loan again due to exposure to the Bed Bath & Beyond store closures. The retailer was the third-largest tenant with 14.4% of the collateral space. The REO asset was liquidated in June 2023.

For 2016, the loan posted a DSCR (NCF) of 1.46x with an occupancy rate of 100%. However, DSCR fell below 1.0x in 2017, with occupancy dropping to 85%. Most recently reported in 2022, the DSCR was -0.21x, with occupancy at 81%. According to servicing commentary, as of June 2023, the center is currently 100% occupied.

The property was originally appraised at $23.0 million at securitization in 2017. The value fell multiple times over the years. Most recently in 2022, the value increased slightly from $5.9 million to $7.4 million.

Liquidation proceeds totaled $10.8 million, and liquidation expenses came in at $4.7 million. This left the asset with about $11 million in losses. Before resolving, the loan represented 25.14% of the remaining collateral behind WBCMT 2007-C33.
